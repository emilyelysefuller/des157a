Post 3 — Visual Thinking

This week made me realize how much I usually rush through images without really thinking about them. The article (“10 Intriguing Photographs to Teach Close Reading and Visual Thinking Skills”) talks about treating images almost like a mystery, where you slow down, look closely, and try to figure out what’s going on instead of jumping to a quick conclusion. I liked the idea of being a “detective” because it makes visual thinking feel more active and engaging, and I think phrased it in a way many could understand.

I saw this a lot in this website I found on a reddit post about cool websites that people enjoy, called "recollection" by "Because Music". At first, before even pressing play, I wasn’t really sure what I was looking at, but that’s kind of the point! It drew me in because it reminded me of the article, where at first I made some assumtions about the site that I actually wasn't too sure about. The visuals pull you in and make you curious, so you want to interact with it and figure it out. Once I pressed play, everything became more dynamic, and I started to understand it with exploration instead of just being told what it is.

I think the biggest strength of the site is how immersive it is. I think it makes you experience the visuals instead of just looking at them. At the same time, it could be a little confusing or overwhelming at first, especially if someone expects something more straightforward. For me, it led me to the conclusion that good visual design doesn’t always explain everything right away, and it can also create curiosity and make you want to explore more.

Post 2 — Overlays Design Pattern Research & Reflections

After reading “Best Practices for Modals / Overlays / Dialog Windows,” I now see how common modals are and how often they can be frustrating when used poorly, I definitely have found them frustrating at times. The strongest point to me was that modals interrupt the user’s flow, so they should only appear when something *really* important needs attention or when input is necessary, such as logging in or confirming an action. If they are overused, users start closing them automatically without even reading them, I know I definitely have.

I also thought the discussion of accessibility was important. I had not considered how keyboard users or low-vision users experience modals. Features like clear close buttons, keyboard navigation, and focus being moved into the dialog can make a major difference in usability. It reminded me that good design is not only about appearance, but about making something functional for as many users as possible.

Post 1 — Form Design & Reflections

After reading the article on form design, I started realizing how many small details actually affect whether someone finishes a form or just gives up. I personally am a person who gives up on forms pretty easily, and will usually find a way around them or just end up not doing them at all. I think one thing that stood out to me the most was how important it is to keep forms simple and only ask for what’s necessary. I’ve definitely clicked out of forms before because they felt too long or overwhelming, and especially if I had to repeat information multiple times, so that really made sense to me. I also liked the point about showing errors right away and clearly explaining how to fix them, because I understand that it can be extremely frustrating to get to the end of a form, long or short, and then get told something is wrong, but being unsure what.

Another takeaway for me was how much structure matters. Grouping related questions and having a clear flow makes everything feel easier and more intuitive. It reminded me of what we’ve been talking about in class with usability also, good design should guide the user without them having to think too hard about it.

A website that I think does a good job with forms is LinkedIn, although it seems kinda heavy with information, I think It’s really straightforward, easy to follow, and gives clear feedback if something is missing. I really love that everything when making your profile or applying to a job is in digestible sections, which I think a lot of forms can lack. I think this article was a great way for me to reflect and be more aware of how thoughtful design can make a simple task feel either smooth or super frustrating.
